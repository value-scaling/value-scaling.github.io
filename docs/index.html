Modern machine learning has seen dramatic gains simply by scaling up models, data, and compute following simple power laws. In supervised learning, these laws predict how loss decreases as you enlarge your model or dataset, enabling practitioners to find the compute-optimal model size for a given budget without costly hyperparameter searches.

On-policy RL methods, like PPO and GRPO, have likewise leveraged scaling to achieve impressive results in simulated environments and language models, but they require fresh training example after every policy update. This limits their sample efficiency in settings where data collection is slow or expensive (e.g. robotics).

By contrast, off-policy RL methods, can in principle learn from any data, regardless of how it was collected. In particular, they can train on the same data multiple times, dramatically boosting sample efficiency by decoupling data collection from policy updates. This capability is essential when each trial costs minutes of wall-clock time or risks hardware damage. However, this strength introduces new challenges for scaling:

  • Two resource axes: RL training hinges on both the amount of environment data D\mathcal DD and the compute C\mathcal CC (FLOPs) spent updating on that data.
  • Non-stationary data: As agents update their policy π\piπ, the distribution of collected experiences shifts, unlike the static datasets of supervised learning.
  • Moving targets: Q-functions QθQ_\thetaQθ​ regress onto bootstrapped targets Qˉ\bar QQˉ​ that change over the course of training, adding instability.

where each gradient step samples a batch of transitions from the replay buffer P\mathcal PP. We can make them sample-efficient by taking multiple gradient steps on the same batch, known as the updates-to-data (UTD) ratio σ\sigmaσ. Increasing the UTD ratio tends to improve performance for small σ\sigmaσ, but we show that pushing the UTD ratio further tends to overfit to stale data on old targets Qˉ\bar QQˉ​ and generalize poorly to newly collected data. Therefore, doing so naively can eventually worsen performance.

In fact, we observe that training with large batch sizes has a similar effect: we see the average sample more times and overfit to those samples, resulting in worse TD error on data recently collected by the policy π\piπ. To counteract this effect at high UTD ratios, we reduce the batch size BBB. Empirically, we find that this inverse rule can be approximated as a power law: B∗(σ)∝σ−αBB^*(\sigma) \propto \sigma^{-\alpha_B}B∗(σ)∝σ−αB​.

We also observe that high UTD ratios lead to plasticity loss, i.e. the inability to fit TD targets appearing later in training, as diagnosed by large parameter norm in the Q-network in prior work. Similarly, we hypothesize that higher learning rates lead to high-magnitude updates against the target, moving the parameters to a state that would suffer from difficulty in fitting subsequent targets. Empirically, we find that larger UTD ratios need smaller learning rates η\etaη, as approximated by a power law: η∗(σ)∝σ−αη\eta^*(\sigma) \propto \sigma^{-\alpha_\eta}η∗(σ)∝σ−αη​.
